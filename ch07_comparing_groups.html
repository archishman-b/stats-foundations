Comparing Groups — t-Tests, ANOVA, and When to Go Nonparametric

Most business hypotheses involve comparing groups: this region vs that one, before vs after, treatment vs control. This chapter covers the statistical tests that formalize those comparisons — and how to choose the right tool for the data type and design you have.

Terms covered: 18 Sections: t-tests · ANOVA · Chi-square · Nonparametric tests · Test selection
Section 7.1

Choosing the right test — a practical decision framework

Statistical test selection is driven by three questions: what type of outcome variable do you have (continuous, binary, count)? How many groups are being compared (2 or more)? Is the data independent across groups or paired/matched? Getting these right determines the correct test — and applying the wrong test produces subtly or obviously wrong results.

Two-Sample t-Test
Tests whether the means of two independent groups are significantly different.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether the observed difference in group means is larger than would be expected by chance given the variability and sample size. The t-test is the standard tool for A/B test analysis with a continuous metric (average revenue, session duration, conversion value). It accounts for both the size of the difference and the noise in the data.
Formula / Rule
t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)Assumptions: independent samples, approximately normal distribution of the test statistic (CLT applies for n > 30 each), and comparable variances (Welch's t-test relaxes the equal variance assumption). Compare t against critical value or compute p-value from t-distribution.
When you see it
In A/B test results for average revenue, session length, order value, load time, and any continuous business metric compared between two independent groups.
Red flag
Ask whether the metric is severely skewed. For right-skewed revenue metrics with small samples (n < 200 per arm), the CLT may not yet apply and Welch's t-test may overstate significance. Use Mann-Whitney or bootstrap for skewed outcomes with limited data.
Business example
A subscription service A/B tests two onboarding flows, measuring days to first value action. Group A: n=850, mean=4.2 days, SD=3.1. Group B: n=840, mean=3.7 days, SD=2.9. t-test: t=2.84, p=0.005. The 0.5-day improvement is statistically significant. The team then checks whether half a day improvement in time-to-value is commercially meaningful enough to ship the change.
Paired t-Test
Compares two measurements from the same subjects — eliminating between-subject variability to sharpen the comparison.
expand
Intuition
It tells you each observation is its own control — you are measuring change within subjects, not comparing different subjects. Using a paired test when the data is truly paired (same store before/after, same customer measured twice) dramatically increases power by removing individual heterogeneity from the error term. Using an independent-samples test on paired data wastes this advantage.
Formula / Rule
t = d̄ / (s_d / √n) where d_i = x_i2 − x_i1Compute the per-subject difference, then run a one-sample t-test on those differences against zero. Requires the differences (not the raw measurements) to be approximately normally distributed.
When you see it
In pre/post studies (same stores before and after a promotion), matched experiment designs (each treatment store paired with a similar control store), and clinical before/after measurements.
Red flag
Ask whether the pairing was determined by the design or added post-hoc. Pairing after data collection to achieve significance is a form of p-hacking — the pairing must be part of the pre-specified design to legitimately use the paired test.
Business example
A retailer measures weekly sales for 50 stores in the week before and week after launching a digital signage system. Paired t-test on the 50 differences yields t=3.12, p=0.003. An independent-samples t-test on the same data yields p=0.14 — non-significant — because between-store variability in sales (which the pairing removes) swamps the within-store signal.
ANOVA — Analysis of Variance
Tests whether means differ across three or more groups by comparing variance between groups to variance within groups.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether at least one group mean differs from the others — but not which ones. ANOVA is the extension of the t-test to three or more groups. Running multiple t-tests instead of ANOVA inflates the family-wise error rate (the multiple testing problem from Chapter 6) — ANOVA tests all groups simultaneously with a single test statistic at the stated α level.
Formula / Rule
F = (Between-group variance) / (Within-group variance)High F means group means differ more than chance would produce. If F is significant: run post-hoc tests (Tukey HSD, Bonferroni) to identify which specific pairs of groups differ. A significant ANOVA only tells you "at least one pair differs" — not where.
When you see it
In multi-variant testing (3+ variants), regional performance comparison, pricing tier analysis, and any analysis comparing a continuous outcome across more than two groups.
Red flag
Ask whether post-hoc tests were run and corrected for multiple comparisons. A significant ANOVA followed by uncorrected pairwise t-tests inflates the false positive rate for the specific pairwise comparisons exactly as much as running multiple t-tests would have.
Business example
A fast food chain tests four promotional offers (A, B, C, D) across stores. ANOVA yields F=6.84, p<0.001 — at least one offer performs differently. Tukey HSD post-hoc tests reveal: A vs D and B vs D are significantly different; C does not significantly differ from any. The chain ranks the offers D > C ≈ B ≈ A and selects D for rollout.
Chi-Square Test
Tests whether observed frequencies in categories differ from expected — the standard test for categorical data independence.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether the distribution of a categorical variable differs across groups — or whether two categorical variables are associated. The test works by comparing observed cell counts in a contingency table to what you would expect if the variables were independent. It is the appropriate tool when the outcome is a category, not a number.
Formula / Rule
χ² = Σ [(Observed − Expected)² / Expected]Build a contingency table (rows = groups, columns = categories). Expected count = (row total × column total) / grand total. Rule of thumb: all expected cell counts must be ≥ 5. Use Fisher's exact test for small samples where this assumption fails.
When you see it
In conversion rate testing (purchased/not purchased by variant), customer segment composition comparisons, demographic breakdowns by group, and any comparison of categorical proportions across groups.
Red flag
Ask whether expected cell counts are all ≥ 5. For rare events or small samples, chi-square provides unreliable results. Fisher's exact test is the appropriate alternative when cells are sparse.
Business example
An insurer checks whether claim filing rate (claim/no claim) differs by distribution channel (direct, broker, comparison site). Chi-square test on a 3×2 contingency table: χ²=18.4, df=2, p<0.001. Channel and claim propensity are not independent — broker channel has significantly higher claim rates, informing underwriting and pricing strategy.
Section 7.2

Non-parametric tests — when distribution assumptions fail

Parametric tests (t-test, ANOVA) assume approximately normal sampling distributions. When this assumption fails — heavily skewed data, small samples, ordinal outcomes, or extreme outliers — non-parametric tests use ranks rather than raw values, making them robust to distributional violations at some cost to power.

When to choose non-parametric Use non-parametric tests when: (1) sample size is small (< 30 per group) and data is clearly non-normal, (2) outcome variable is ordinal (satisfaction ratings, risk bands), (3) data has extreme outliers that cannot be removed, (4) comparison is of medians rather than means. If sample size is large (> 100 per group), CLT makes parametric tests robust even for skewed data — non-parametric tests offer little advantage and lose some power.
Mann-Whitney U Test
Non-parametric alternative to the two-sample t-test — tests whether one group's values tend to be larger than the other's using ranks.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether one group consistently produces higher values than another — without assuming any particular distribution. The test replaces raw values with their ranks across both groups combined, then checks whether the ranks are randomly mixed or systematically concentrated in one group.
Formula / Rule
U = n₁n₂ + n₁(n₁+1)/2 − R₁Rank all observations from both groups combined. U counts the number of times a value from group 1 precedes a value from group 2 in the ranking. Large U (or small U) indicates one group tends to dominate. For large samples, U is approximately normal.
When you see it
In A/B tests with right-skewed metrics and moderate sample sizes, clinical outcome comparisons where normality fails, and satisfaction score comparisons between groups.
Red flag
Ask whether statistical significance came from the ranks shifting or from the top quantile behaving differently. Mann-Whitney tests the central tendency of the rank distribution — it may miss important changes that are concentrated in the top 10% of the distribution.
Business example
A gaming company tests two monetization prompts measuring revenue per user. Revenue is highly right-skewed with 90% of users paying nothing. With n=300 per arm, CLT is insufficient for the mean. Mann-Whitney U test shows no significant difference in the rank distribution (p=0.41) — but a separate analysis of top-decile spenders shows the new prompt generates 23% more from high spenders. The tests are answering different questions.
Kruskal-Wallis Test
Non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA — tests whether at least one group tends to produce higher ranks than others.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether the rank distributions of three or more groups are consistent with all being drawn from the same population. It is the natural extension of Mann-Whitney to multiple groups, and the appropriate choice when ANOVA assumptions are violated in a multi-group comparison.
Formula / Rule
H = [12/N(N+1)] × Σ(Rⱼ²/nⱼ) − 3(N+1)Rank all observations from all groups. Compute the rank sum for each group. H follows a chi-square distribution with k−1 degrees of freedom. Significant result: at least one group differs. Follow up with pairwise Dunn tests with multiple comparison correction.
When you see it
In comparing satisfaction scores, NPS distributions, or skewed performance metrics across 3+ customer segments, regions, or product tiers.
Red flag
Ask whether the test was followed by appropriate post-hoc pairwise comparisons. A significant Kruskal-Wallis test only identifies that differences exist somewhere — not where.
Business example
A logistics company compares driver satisfaction scores across five regions on a 1–10 ordinal scale. ANOVA would assume equal-interval scaling of the ordinal ratings. Kruskal-Wallis (H=14.3, p=0.006) reveals significant differences across regions. Dunn post-hoc tests identify Region 3 vs Region 1 and Region 5 vs Region 1 as the significant pairs driving the overall result.
Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test
Non-parametric alternative to the paired t-test — tests whether matched differences are systematically positive or negative using signed ranks.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether the within-subject differences tend to be positive or negative — the paired version of Mann-Whitney. It ranks the absolute values of the differences and then checks whether the positive and negative differences are balanced. More powerful than Mann-Whitney when pairing genuinely reduces noise, and robust when the differences are skewed.
Formula / Rule
Compute per-pair differences. Rank by absolute value. Sum positive ranks (T+) and negative ranks (T−). Under H₀ of no difference, T+ and T− should be roughly equal. The test statistic is min(T+, T−) compared to critical values. Zero differences are excluded.
When you see it
In before/after studies with small samples or non-normal differences, matched experiment designs, and clinical repeated measurements where normality cannot be assumed.
Red flag
Ask whether there are many zero differences in the data. The Wilcoxon test excludes tied zero differences, which can reduce effective sample size substantially and affect power when a large portion of pairs show no change.
Business example
A healthcare provider tests whether a care pathway change reduces 30-day readmission rates, matching 40 hospitals by size and acuity. Readmission differences are skewed right. Wilcoxon signed-rank: W=112, p=0.028 — the care pathway significantly reduces readmissions across matched pairs, consistent with what a paired t-test would have found but more robust to the asymmetric distribution of differences.
Section 7.3

Variance decomposition and effect attribution

ANOVA is more than a test — it is a framework for partitioning total variability into explained and unexplained components. Understanding this decomposition connects hypothesis testing to regression, experimental design, and model evaluation.

R² and Eta-Squared (η²)
The proportion of total variability explained by group membership — the effect size analogue for ANOVA.
expand
Intuition
It tells you not just that groups differ, but how much of the total outcome variation is attributable to group differences. η² = 0.10 means 10% of variance in the outcome is explained by which group an observation belongs to. This is the ANOVA equivalent of R² in regression and provides the effect size needed to assess practical significance.
Formula / Rule
η² = SS_between / SS_totalCohen's conventions: η² ≈ 0.01 = small effect, 0.06 = medium, 0.14 = large. Partial η² controls for other factors in factorial ANOVA. With large samples, even η² = 0.001 can be statistically significant — always report alongside F-statistic and p-value.
When you see it
In ANOVA result tables, multi-variant test reports, and experimental analysis where the goal is to quantify how much a factor explains outcome variance.
Red flag
Ask for η² alongside the F-statistic. A highly significant F with η² = 0.003 means the group difference is real but explains almost nothing commercially — the effect may be statistically detectable but operationally irrelevant.
Business example
A retail chain tests three store layouts with 200 stores each. ANOVA: F=12.3, p<0.001, η²=0.04. The layout explains 4% of sales variability — statistically significant, medium effect by convention. But store location, footfall, and demographics explain far more variance than layout does. The team concludes layout matters at the margin but is not a primary sales driver.
Test Assumptions and Robustness
The conditions under which a statistical test's results are valid — and how far violations can be tolerated before switching to alternatives.
expand
Intuition
It tells you statistical tests are not universally valid — they produce reliable results only under stated conditions. Violating assumptions does not always break the test badly, but it can invalidate p-values, inflate Type I errors, or reduce power. Knowing which assumptions matter most for which violations is what makes test selection practically useful.
Formula / Rule
t-test critical assumptions: (1) Independence of observations — most important, rarely robust to violation. (2) Approximate normality of sampling distribution — robust for n > 30 (CLT). (3) Equal variances — use Welch's t-test to relax this. ANOVA adds: (4) Homogeneity of variances across groups — use Welch's ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis if violated.
When you see it
In methodology audits, experimental design reviews, and any analysis where results seem counterintuitive or where assumptions need to be documented for governance purposes.
Red flag
Ask whether independence of observations holds. Customers from the same household, stores in the same region, or patients from the same hospital cluster — violating independence inflates effective sample size and understates p-values. Clustered standard errors or mixed models are required.
Business example
A retailer tests a promotion across 200 stores but 15 regional managers each influence 8–20 stores within their region. Stores within the same region are not independent — their sales correlate through regional manager decisions, local events, and shared demographics. A standard ANOVA treating all 200 stores as independent units overstates significance. Clustering standard errors at the regional level gives the honest result.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 7 cheat sheet

Test selection quick reference
Two independent groups, continuous outcome
Welch's t-test (default). Mann-Whitney if small n and severely skewed. Bootstrap for composite or non-standard metrics.
Paired/matched observations, continuous
Paired t-test. Wilcoxon signed-rank if differences are non-normal. Pairing must be pre-specified, not chosen post-hoc.
Three or more groups, continuous
One-way ANOVA + Tukey post-hoc. Kruskal-Wallis if ANOVA assumptions fail. Never run multiple t-tests — inflate Type I error.
Categorical outcome, 2+ groups
Chi-square test. Fisher's exact test for sparse cells (expected count < 5). Z-test for proportions for two-group binary outcomes.
Report effect size, not just p-value
Always accompany test results with η², Cohen's d, or business-unit effect. Statistical significance is necessary but not sufficient for a decision.
Check independence of observations
Clustering by region, manager, or household violates independence. Cluster standard errors or use mixed models when observations are grouped.
