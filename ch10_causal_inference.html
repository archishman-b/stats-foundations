Causal Inference and Experimental Design

The difference between a business that learns from data and one that merely collects it is causal thinking. This chapter covers how to establish that an intervention actually worked — using randomised experiments when possible and quasi-experimental methods when not.

Terms covered: 19 Sections: RCT · Potential outcomes · DiD · IV · RD · A/B testing · Uplift
Section 10.1

The fundamental problem of causal inference

We can never observe the same unit in both the treated and untreated state simultaneously. A customer either received the discount or did not — we cannot know what they would have done under the alternative. All causal inference is solving this problem: constructing a credible estimate of the counterfactual — what would have happened without the intervention.

Potential Outcomes Framework
The formal framework for causal inference: each unit has two potential outcomes — one under treatment, one under control — and only one is ever observed.
expand
Intuition
It tells you causal inference is a missing data problem. For every customer, there exists a potential outcome under treatment (Y₁) and under control (Y₀). The individual treatment effect is Y₁ − Y₀. We can only observe one of these — the other is the counterfactual. The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) is the population average of (Y₁ − Y₀). Randomization makes Y₁ and Y₀ estimable from group averages.
Formula / Rule
ATE = E[Y₁ − Y₀] = E[Y₁] − E[Y₀]Under random assignment: E[Y₁|treated] = E[Y₁] and E[Y₀|control] = E[Y₀]. The groups are exchangeable — treated units represent what control units would have looked like under treatment. This is why randomization identifies causal effects from observed group differences.
When you see it
In experimental design methodology, programme evaluation, A/B test statistical frameworks, and any formal discussion of treatment effect estimation.
Red flag
Ask whether the SUTVA assumption holds — that one unit's treatment does not affect another's outcome (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption). Network effects, spillovers, and social influence all violate SUTVA, making standard ATE estimates biased.
Business example
A marketplace tests a seller incentive programme. SUTVA is violated if treated sellers compete directly with control sellers — improving treated sellers may reduce control seller sales simply through competition redistribution, not programme effectiveness. The estimated ATE would overstate impact by treating competitive shifts as treatment effects.
Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT)
An experiment where units are randomly assigned to treatment or control — the gold standard for establishing causation.
expand
Intuition
It tells you random assignment eliminates confounding by distributing all unobserved characteristics equally across groups — in expectation. No need to measure or control for every possible confounder; randomization handles them all simultaneously. The resulting observed difference in outcomes between groups estimates the causal effect, not just an association.
Formula / Rule
Causal effect = ȳ_treated − ȳ_controlThis simple difference estimates the ATE under randomization. Conditions for a valid RCT: (1) random assignment, (2) no contamination between groups, (3) no differential attrition (dropout), (4) pre-registered primary endpoint. Blocking (stratifying randomization by key variables) reduces variance without affecting unbiasedness.
When you see it
In A/B tests, clinical trials, programme evaluations, policy pilots, and any design where causal attribution is required and random assignment is feasible.
Red flag
Ask whether randomisation was verified. Check that pre-experiment characteristics are balanced between groups — a t-test on key baseline variables should yield p > 0.05 for a well-randomized experiment. Imbalance suggests contamination or implementation error.
Business example
A fintech lender runs a randomised trial of credit limit increases on a random 20% sample of eligible customers. Pre-randomisation balance checks confirm comparable income, utilisation, and risk scores between groups. The resulting spend increase and default rate change in the treated group minus the control group estimates the causal effect of the limit increase — free of selection bias by design.
Attrition and Non-Compliance
Dropout from an experiment or failure to take the assigned treatment — both can invalidate causal conclusions even from a randomised design.
expand
Intuition
It tells you randomization is only the beginning — how the experiment is administered matters too. If treated units opt out at higher rates than controls (differential attrition), the remaining groups are no longer comparable. If not everyone takes up the assigned treatment (non-compliance), the naive treated-vs-control comparison estimates something different from the intended ATE.
Formula / Rule
Intent-to-treat (ITT): compare assigned groups regardless of whether they actually received treatment — preserves randomization but dilutes estimated effect. Per-protocol: compare those who actually received treatment vs control — risks selection bias from non-random non-compliance. Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE / IV estimator): estimates effect for "compliers" — those who take treatment if and only if assigned to it.
When you see it
In clinical trials (patient dropout), consumer product A/B tests (users switching apps mid-test), workplace training evaluations (voluntary non-attendance), and policy evaluations with imperfect rollout.
Red flag
Ask what the attrition rate was and whether it differed between groups. Differential attrition rates above a few percent can bias results meaningfully. Report ITT estimates as the primary result and note they represent a lower bound on the true treatment effect for compliers.
Business example
A SaaS company tests a new onboarding programme. 35% of treated users switch to the old onboarding before completing — the treatment takeup rate is 65%. ITT analysis (all treated vs all control) shows +4.2% activation. LATE estimate adjusts for non-compliance: true effect for completers ≈ +4.2%/0.65 = +6.5%. The naive ITT understates the true programme effect for those who actually participate.
Section 10.2

Quasi-experimental methods — when randomization is not possible

Most business interventions cannot be randomized after the fact — a policy already implemented, a product already launched, an acquisition already completed. Quasi-experimental methods use naturally occurring variation to approximate the counterfactual without random assignment. They require strong assumptions, but when those assumptions hold, they provide credible causal estimates from observational data.

Difference-in-Differences (DiD)
Estimates a causal effect by comparing outcome changes over time in treated vs control groups — using the control group's trend as the counterfactual.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the treatment effect is estimated as: (change in treated group) minus (change in control group). The control group's trend proxies what the treated group would have done without the intervention. This "parallel trends" assumption — that both groups would have followed the same trajectory in the absence of treatment — is the key identifying assumption.
Formula / Rule
DiD = (Ȳ_treated,post − Ȳ_treated,pre) − (Ȳ_control,post − Ȳ_control,pre)Equivalently: regress outcome on treatment indicator, time indicator, and their interaction. The interaction coefficient is the DiD estimate. Critical assumption: parallel pre-trends — verify by testing whether trends diverged before treatment. Failure of parallel trends invalidates the estimate.
When you see it
In policy evaluations (minimum wage effects), geographic rollouts (new store impact on regional sales), platform feature launches affecting one user cohort, and natural experiment analyses.
Red flag
Ask for the pre-trend plot. If treated and control groups were diverging before the intervention, parallel trends cannot be assumed and the DiD estimate will conflate pre-existing differences with treatment effects.
Business example
A grocery chain opens delivery service in 5 cities and uses 5 similar non-expansion cities as controls. Pre-expansion: both groups show flat sales trends. Post-expansion: treated cities grow 12%; control cities grow 3%. DiD estimate: +9 percentage points attributable to delivery launch. The parallel trends assumption is supported by the flat pre-period, giving credibility to the causal attribution.
Instrumental Variables (IV)
Uses a variable that affects treatment but has no direct effect on the outcome to isolate the causal variation in the treatment.
expand
Intuition
It tells you causal identification is achieved by finding a source of variation in treatment that is "as good as random" — unrelated to confounders. A valid instrument creates a natural experiment within observational data. The classic example: military draft lottery as instrument for education (randomly assigned draft risk affects years served which affects civilian earnings — but lottery number has no direct earnings effect).
Formula / Rule
LATE = Cov(Y, Z) / Cov(D, Z) [where Z = instrument, D = treatment]Two requirements for a valid instrument: (1) Relevance — Z must be correlated with D (first stage F > 10). (2) Exclusion restriction — Z affects Y only through D, not directly. The exclusion restriction cannot be tested — it must be argued on substantive grounds. Weak instruments (F < 10) produce severely biased IV estimates.
When you see it
In academic economic research, programme evaluations where treatment is non-random, and any analysis where an exogenous source of variation in treatment can be credibly defended.
Red flag
Ask whether the exclusion restriction can be defended. The instrument must affect the outcome only through the treatment — any direct path invalidates the instrument. This assumption is often assumed rather than demonstrated, making IV results in observational research frequently contested.
Business example
A streaming platform wants to estimate causal effect of watching a specific genre on subscription renewal. Genre preference is confounded by overall engagement. An instrument: whether the first recommended video in a new user's session happened to be from that genre (driven by recommender randomness, not user preference). First stage: genre exposure predicts subsequent genre watching. LATE estimates the causal renewal effect for users influenced by the initial recommendation.
Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD)
Exploits a threshold rule that assigns treatment discontinuously — units just above and below the threshold are used as natural treatment and control groups.
expand
Intuition
It tells you that units just below a cutoff score (not treated) are excellent counterfactuals for units just above it (treated) because they are otherwise similar — they differ primarily in whether they crossed the threshold. The causal effect is estimated as the discontinuous jump in outcomes at the threshold. The key assumption is that potential outcomes vary smoothly around the cutoff — no other discontinuous change at exactly that point.
Formula / Rule
RDD effect = lim_{x↓c} E[Y|X=x] − lim_{x↑c} E[Y|X=x]Estimate the jump at the threshold c using local linear regression on both sides. Bandwidth selection is critical — too narrow gives high variance, too wide introduces bias from units far from the threshold. Check for manipulation: if agents can precisely control their running variable to select treatment, the design is invalid (McCrary density test).
When you see it
In credit limit assignment (score threshold), scholarship award (grade cutoff), medication dosage rules, and any policy with a sharp eligibility threshold based on a measurable criterion.
Red flag
Ask whether units could have manipulated their running variable to cross the threshold. If applicants know the cutoff and can time or adjust their applications to qualify, the design is contaminated — units near the threshold are no longer similar above and below it.
Business example
A lender approves all applicants scoring above 650 on their internal model. Comparing customers scoring 645–649 (declined) with those scoring 650–655 (approved) estimates the causal effect of loan access. Near the threshold, the only systematic difference between groups is approval status — other characteristics (income, employment, intent) should be continuous through 650, making the comparison locally unconfounded.
Uplift Modeling
Predicts the causal effect of treatment for each individual — identifying who actually benefits, unlike standard models that predict outcome regardless of treatment.
expand
Intuition
It tells you that the population of customers is not homogeneous in their response to an intervention. Four types exist: Persuadables (benefit from treatment — the ones you want to target), Sure Things (buy regardless — waste of marketing budget), Lost Causes (will not buy regardless — also wasted budget), and Sleeping Dogs (actually harmed by treatment — the most dangerous). Standard outcome models find all of these — uplift models isolate persuadables.
Formula / Rule
Uplift = P(Y=1|T=1, X) − P(Y=1|T=0, X)Two-model approach (T-learner): train separate outcome models for treated and control, then score each unit on both and take the difference. Requires randomised training data with treatment indicator. Evaluate with Qini curve or uplift by decile table — targeting top uplift deciles maximizes incremental ROI.
When you see it
In retention marketing (who is genuinely at-risk and responsive to outreach), promotional targeting (who is swayed by discounts vs who would buy anyway), and any CRM intervention where heterogeneous treatment effects matter.
Red flag
Ask whether the uplift model was evaluated on held-out data with an Qini curve, not accuracy. Evaluating uplift models with standard classification metrics is meaningless — the counterfactual is never observed. Qini measures incremental lift over random selection.
Business example
A subscription company runs churn retention campaigns on all at-risk customers. An uplift model trained on a randomised experiment reveals: 40% of targeted customers were "sure things" who would have stayed anyway (discount wasted), 15% were "sleeping dogs" who were more likely to churn after receiving a promotional message (discount actively harmful), and only 30% were genuine persuadables. Targeting only persuadables reduces retention spend 55% while maintaining the same incremental retention outcome.
Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM)
When the actual allocation of users to experiment arms differs from the intended split — a sign of implementation error that invalidates causal conclusions.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the experiment was not implemented as designed — users were not randomised as intended. If you designed a 50/50 split and see 52/48, the randomiser may have a bug, a caching issue may be re-routing users, or bot traffic may be falling disproportionately into one arm. SRM means the treatment and control groups may not be comparable and the causal estimate is unreliable.
Formula / Rule
Chi-square test: χ² = Σ(observed − expected)² / expectedCheck whether the observed split matches the intended allocation. For a 50/50 split with n=10,000: expected 5,000 each. If observed is 5,300/4,700 without an intentional reason, investigate before interpreting results. Small p-value on the SRM chi-square test = allocation problem.
When you see it
As a standard experiment health check in mature A/B testing platforms, and in post-hoc diagnosis when experiment results are counterintuitive or inconsistent.
Red flag
Ask whether SRM checks were run before results were interpreted. Running significance tests on a biased experiment produces statistically precise but causally meaningless estimates. SRM should be checked before any results are communicated.
Business example
A mobile app A/B test targeting 50/50 split shows 58/42 in actual delivery. Investigation reveals a caching bug on iOS was keeping some users on the control experience while logging them as treated. The "treated" group contained a disproportionate share of Android users — who have systematically different engagement patterns. All experiment results for that period are discarded.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 10 cheat sheet

Key principles from this chapter
Causal inference is a missing data problem
The counterfactual is never observed. Every causal method is a different strategy for constructing a credible estimate of what would have happened without the intervention.
Randomization eliminates confounding
Random assignment makes groups comparable on all observed and unobserved characteristics in expectation. It is the most reliable causal identification strategy when feasible.
Parallel trends must be verified for DiD
Check pre-period trends before interpreting DiD estimates. Groups diverging before treatment invalidates the control group as a counterfactual.
IV requires a credible exclusion restriction
The instrument must affect the outcome only through the treatment. This cannot be statistically tested — it must be justified substantively. Contested exclusion restrictions make IV results contestable.
Always check SRM before reporting
An experiment with wrong allocation ratios was not properly randomized. Interpret results only after confirming actual splits match intended splits.
Uplift models find persuadables
Standard outcome models predict who buys — not who buys because of treatment. Uplift models identify the segment where treatment has positive incremental effect, avoiding wasted spend on sure things and sleeping dogs.
