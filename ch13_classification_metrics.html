Classification Metrics — Beyond Accuracy

Accuracy is almost never the right metric for business classification models. When outcomes are rare, classes are asymmetric in cost, or ranking matters more than labeling, a whole toolkit of metrics — precision, recall, AUC, Gini, KS — becomes essential. This chapter makes them intuitive and usable.

Section 13.1

The confusion matrix — the foundation of all classification metrics

Every classification metric derives from the same 2×2 table of actual versus predicted outcomes. Understanding this table deeply makes the relationships between all derived metrics clear — and makes it obvious why some metrics are appropriate for some business goals and not others.

Predicted Positive Predicted Negative
Actual Positive True Positive (TP)Correctly flagged as positive. Model is right, event occurred. False Negative (FN)Missed positive. Event occurred but model said no.
Actual Negative False Positive (FP)False alarm. Model said yes but event did not occur. True Negative (TN)Correctly passed as negative. Model is right, no event.
Precision
Of all predicted positives, what fraction were actually positive? Measures false alarm rate among model flags.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how trustworthy a positive prediction is. Precision = 0.30 means 70% of fraud alerts are false alarms. Precision = 0.85 means 85% of the model's positive predictions are correct. High precision is most important when the cost of acting on a false alarm is high — contacting a customer who does not need retention outreach, investigating a legitimate transaction.
Formula / Rule
Precision = TP / (TP + FP)Increasing the decision threshold increases precision (fewer false alarms) at the cost of lower recall (more misses). At threshold=1.0: precision=1.0, recall=0.0. The precision-recall tradeoff is the core of threshold selection in business applications.
When you see it
In fraud alert prioritisation, customer outreach targeting, medical diagnosis, content recommendation, and any context where the cost of a false alarm is high.
Red flag
Ask what precision is at the operational threshold, not just at AUC level. A model with AUC=0.92 deployed at a threshold that generates 3,000 alerts per day may have precision of only 12% if the fraud rate is 1% — 2,640 legitimate transactions are being disrupted daily.
Business example
A card issuer's fraud model has precision of 0.34 at the operating threshold. Analysts review 1,000 alerts per day; 340 are genuine fraud; 660 are false positives. The team's target is precision ≥ 0.50 — achieved by raising the threshold, which drops daily alert volume to 620 with 310 true fraud (same fraud caught, half the manual review workload).
Recall (Sensitivity / True Positive Rate)
Of all actual positives, what fraction were correctly identified? Measures how much of the target the model catches.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how much of the event the model captures. Recall = 0.80 means 80% of all actual fraud cases are flagged — 20% slip through. High recall is most important when the cost of missing a positive is high — a missed cancer diagnosis, an undetected fraud transaction, a churning customer who was never contacted. The tradeoff: increasing recall usually decreases precision.
Formula / Rule
Recall = TP / (TP + FN)Also called sensitivity or true positive rate. Used alongside specificity (true negative rate = TN/(TN+FP)) in diagnostic testing. Recall of 1.0 means all positives are found — but at threshold 0, precision would be equal to the base rate (all observations flagged).
When you see it
In fraud detection, cancer screening, churn prevention, compliance monitoring, and any context where missing a positive outcome has high cost.
Red flag
Ask what recall is at the operational threshold and what the cost of a miss is. A fraud model with 0.90 precision but 0.30 recall is catching only 30% of fraud — the 70% it misses may far exceed the cost of the false alarms the team is working to eliminate.
Business example
A healthcare payer's readmission risk model has recall of 0.65 at its current threshold — meaning 35% of readmissions are not flagged for care management intervention. The medical director calculates that each missed readmission costs $4,200 in avoidable expense. Lowering the threshold raises recall to 0.82 at the cost of a precision drop from 0.72 to 0.58 — generating more care management calls, most of which are still genuinely useful interventions.
F1 Score and Fβ
The harmonic mean of precision and recall — a single-number metric that balances both, with Fβ allowing weighting toward the more important one.
expand
Intuition
It tells you a combined measure that penalises extreme imbalances between precision and recall. F1 = 0 if either precision or recall is 0, and F1 = 1 only when both are perfect. The harmonic mean is more sensitive to the lower of the two values than the arithmetic mean — making it harder to game by maximizing one at the expense of the other.
Formula / Rule
F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)Fβ = (1+β²) × P × R / (β²P + R). β=2 weights recall twice as heavily (useful when misses are costly). β=0.5 weights precision twice as heavily (useful when false alarms are costly). F1 assumes equal cost — rarely valid in business. Fβ makes the cost asymmetry explicit.
When you see it
In NLP model evaluation, medical diagnosis benchmarking, and any application where a single metric combining precision and recall is needed for model comparison or threshold selection.
Red flag
Ask whether F1 is the right balance for the business context. F1 assumes equal weighting of false positives and false negatives — which is almost never true. F2 or F0.5 may better reflect actual operational cost asymmetry.
Business example
A cybersecurity firm compares two intrusion detection models. Model A: Precision=0.90, Recall=0.60, F1=0.72. Model B: Precision=0.70, Recall=0.85, F2=0.82. For critical infrastructure monitoring where every missed intrusion is catastrophic, Model B's higher recall (and F2) makes it operationally superior — even though Model A has better F1.
Section 13.2

Ranking metrics — AUC, Gini, and KS

Many business models are used for ranking rather than labeling. A churn model scores customers so the top decile can be contacted first — the exact threshold does not matter, only whether the model correctly orders them. AUC, Gini, and KS statistics all measure ranking quality, independent of any specific threshold.

AUC-ROC — Area Under the ROC Curve
The probability that a randomly selected positive ranks higher than a randomly selected negative — a threshold-independent measure of ranking quality.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how well the model separates positives from negatives across all possible thresholds. AUC = 0.5 is random chance (coin flip). AUC = 1.0 is perfect separation. AUC = 0.85 means: pick a random positive and a random negative — there is an 85% chance the model ranks the positive higher. For business models, AUC above 0.80 is typically considered strong; 0.70–0.80 is usable; below 0.70 questions whether the model adds value over heuristics.
Formula / Rule
AUC = P(score(pos) > score(neg))The ROC curve plots TPR (recall) vs FPR (1-specificity) at all thresholds. AUC is the area under this curve. Weakness: AUC averages performance across all thresholds equally, including very low and very high thresholds that may never be used operationally. PR-AUC (precision-recall area) is often more informative for rare events.
When you see it
In credit scorecard evaluation, churn model validation, fraud model performance reports, and any binary classification model comparison.
Red flag
Ask whether AUC was computed on temporal holdout data or random split data. For time series applications, AUC from a random split is optimistic — the model was tested on contemporaneous data it would never have access to in production.
Business example
A consumer lender benchmarks its application fraud model: AUC improves from 0.838 (logistic regression, legacy) to 0.892 (gradient boosting, new). The 5.4-point AUC improvement represents better rank ordering of fraud risk across all customers. At the same false positive rate (5%), the new model detects 18% more fraud — estimated at £1.4M additional fraud prevented annually at current application volumes.
Gini Coefficient
Rescaled AUC: Gini = 2×AUC − 1. The standard performance metric for credit scorecards, ranging from 0 (random) to 1 (perfect).
expand
Intuition
It tells you the same discrimination quality as AUC but on a 0–1 scale where 0 is "no better than random" and 1 is "perfect." The credit industry standardised on Gini because the 0–1 scale is more intuitive for governance reporting — "our scorecard has Gini 0.63" is easier to benchmark than "AUC 0.815."
Formula / Rule
Gini = 2 × AUC − 1Credit scorecard benchmarks: Gini >0.60 = strong, 0.45–0.60 = acceptable, <0.45 = weak. Gini is also a measure of income inequality (Lorenz curve area) — same mathematical concept, different application. In model governance: Gini is tracked monthly; declines of >5 Gini points trigger review.
When you see it
In credit scorecard documentation, model performance reports for lenders and insurers, and any regulatory capital model validation context where AUC is the underlying metric.
Red flag
Ask whether Gini is being compared across models trained on different populations. A Gini of 0.58 in a prime lending population and 0.58 in a subprime population represent very different levels of actual predictive value — the subprime population has more heterogeneity, making separation easier.
Business example
A bank's annual scorecard governance review shows Gini declining from 0.64 to 0.55 over 18 months. This 9-point decline triggers a redevelopment project. Investigation reveals the decline is driven by a new digital acquisition channel (different customer risk profile) that the model was not trained on — a combination of data drift and concept drift requiring full model rebuild.
KS Statistic
The maximum separation between the cumulative distributions of positive and negative scores — measures the best possible discrimination point.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the single threshold where the model most cleanly separates positives from negatives. KS = 0.42 means at the optimal threshold, 42% more positives than negatives are correctly classified. KS is useful as a quick diagnostic and threshold-selection tool, but it summarises only the best separation point — unlike AUC which averages across all thresholds.
Formula / Rule
KS = max|F_positive(t) − F_negative(t)| over all thresholds tPlot cumulative % of positives and negatives captured at each score threshold. KS is the maximum vertical gap between these two curves. The KS threshold is a natural operating point candidate — not always optimal, but often a reasonable starting point for threshold selection.
When you see it
In credit scorecard validation tables, model comparison reports, and threshold selection analyses in the financial services industry.
Red flag
Ask whether the KS threshold is operationally feasible. The KS-optimal threshold maximizes separation but may correspond to a recall or precision level that is commercially impractical — the operating threshold should be selected based on business cost function, not purely KS maximization.
Business example
A credit model shows KS=0.48 at a score cutoff of 620. At this cutoff: 74% of bad loans are above the cutoff (would be declined), and only 26% of good loans are above the cutoff (would be incorrectly declined). The team uses KS to identify the approximate cutoff zone, then fine-tunes using expected loss optimisation to find the profit-maximizing threshold within 10 score points of the KS point.
Calibration
Whether predicted probabilities match actual outcome rates — a model that predicts 30% should be right 30% of the time among those predictions.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether the model's probabilities can be trusted as actual probabilities rather than just relative rankings. A perfectly calibrated model: among all predictions of 20%, 20% of cases actually experience the event. Most models are well-calibrated for ranking but poorly calibrated in absolute terms — gradient boosting and random forests are typically poorly calibrated out of the box.
Formula / Rule
Calibration plot (reliability diagram): bin predicted probabilities into deciles. Plot (mean predicted probability, observed event rate) for each bin. Perfect calibration: all points on the diagonal. Brier score: mean squared error of probability predictions (lower = better). Calibration can be corrected with isotonic regression or Platt scaling post-training.
When you see it
In credit default provisioning (must calculate expected loss from predicted probabilities), insurance pricing, medical risk assessment, and any business use case where probabilities are multiplied by costs to make financial decisions.
Red flag
Ask whether calibration was checked separately from discrimination (AUC/Gini). A model can rank perfectly (AUC=0.95) but be badly miscalibrated — reporting 50% default probability when the true rate is 8%. Using miscalibrated probabilities in expected loss calculations will over-provision by 6× and misallocate capital.
Business example
A lender's gradient boosting default model has Gini=0.71 — excellent discrimination. But the calibration plot shows systematic overstatement of probabilities: at predicted 40% default probability, actual rate is 22%. The model is recalibrated using Platt scaling on a held-out calibration set — bringing predicted probabilities within 2% of actual rates across all deciles, making the model suitable for regulatory expected loss calculation.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 13 cheat sheet

Classification metrics quick reference
Accuracy: almost never the right metric
A model predicting "not fraud" for everyone is 99.8% accurate when fraud rate is 0.2%. Use precision, recall, or AUC instead.
Precision: cost of false alarms
TP/(TP+FP). Matters most when investigating a false positive is expensive — fraud review teams, medical follow-up, customer outreach.
Recall: cost of misses
TP/(TP+FN). Matters most when missing a positive is costly — undetected fraud, missed diagnosis, uncontacted churner.
AUC: ranking quality across all thresholds
0.5 = random, 1.0 = perfect. AUC > 0.95 on real data almost always signals leakage. Evaluate on temporal holdout for time series models.
Gini = 2×AUC − 1
Industry standard for credit models. Gini > 0.60 = strong. Monitor monthly; >5 point decline triggers model review.
Calibration ≠ discrimination
A model can rank perfectly but produce wrong probabilities. Always check calibration separately if probabilities will be used in financial calculations.
