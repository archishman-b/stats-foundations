Correlation, Association, and Common Traps

Correlation is the most used and most abused concept in business analytics. This chapter covers what it measures, what it does not measure, and the specific ways it misleads — from spurious correlations to the ecological fallacy to the dangers of range restriction.

Terms covered: 16 Sections: Pearson r · Spearman · Other measures · Confounding · Spurious correlations · Traps
Section 8.1

Measuring association — the right tool for the data type

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables. But "correlation" as a concept covers many different measures, each appropriate for different data types and relationship structures. Using the wrong measure produces numbers that look precise but measure the wrong thing.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)
Measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. Ranges from −1 to +1.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how closely two variables track together in a straight-line relationship. r = +1 is perfect positive linear relationship. r = 0 is no linear relationship. r = −1 is perfect negative linear relationship. Crucially: r = 0 does not mean no relationship at all — a perfect quadratic relationship can produce r = 0. Pearson measures linear association only.
Formula / Rule
r = Σ[(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)] / (n−1)sₓsᵧCorrelation is scale-invariant — multiplying all x or y values by a constant does not change r. Interpretation: |r| < 0.1 negligible; 0.1–0.3 small; 0.3–0.5 moderate; 0.5–0.7 large; > 0.7 very large (these are loose conventions — business context should define what is actionable).
When you see it
In feature selection, data exploration, dashboard KPI relationships, risk factor analysis, and any exploratory data analysis comparing two continuous variables.
Red flag
Ask whether the relationship could be non-linear. Always plot a scatterplot — a correlation of 0.3 with a clear non-linear pattern is more interesting than a linear r of 0.5 suggests.
Business example
An insurer finds r = 0.62 between vehicle age and claim frequency. The relationship is positive, moderately strong, and linear enough for Pearson to be meaningful. But further investigation reveals the relationship is stronger for years 3–8 and weaker beyond year 10 — the overall r masks a non-linear age effect that a piecewise model would capture better.
Spearman Rank Correlation (ρ)
Measures the monotonic relationship between two variables using their ranks — robust to outliers and non-linearity.
expand
Intuition
It tells you whether one variable tends to increase as the other increases — without requiring the relationship to be exactly linear. Spearman applies Pearson's formula to the ranks of the data rather than the raw values, making it resistant to extreme values and appropriate for ordinal data. It captures monotonic relationships: X going up as Y goes up, even if the rate is not constant.
Formula / Rule
ρ = 1 − [6Σdᵢ²] / [n(n²−1)] where dᵢ = rank differenceReplace each value with its rank. Compute Pearson correlation on the ranks. Interpreting ρ: same conventions as r. Use Spearman when: (1) data is ordinal, (2) relationship is monotonic but non-linear, (3) outliers are present that would distort Pearson, (4) small samples from non-normal distributions.
When you see it
In model feature screening with mixed variable types, NPS-to-outcome correlations, non-normal financial data analysis, and any correlation analysis where outliers are a concern.
Red flag
Ask whether the Pearson and Spearman correlations differ substantially. A large gap between r and ρ signals that outliers or non-linearity are driving the Pearson result — and the Spearman is the more reliable measure of the underlying relationship.
Business example
A media company measures relationship between articles shared (highly right-skewed — a few viral pieces dominate) and new subscriber acquisition. Pearson r = 0.71 — but driven by five viral outliers. Spearman ρ = 0.38 — the rank correlation after extreme values are moderated. The Spearman is the better description of the typical relationship between sharing and subscriptions.
Cramér's V
Measures the strength of association between two categorical variables — the chi-square-based correlation analogue for non-numeric data.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how strongly two categorical variables are associated — normalizing the chi-square statistic so that 0 means no association and 1 means perfect association, regardless of table size. It is the standard effect size for categorical-categorical relationships, complementing the chi-square test's p-value with a measure of how strong the association is.
Formula / Rule
V = √(χ² / (n × min(r−1, c−1)))Where n = total count, r = rows, c = columns. V ranges from 0 to 1. Interpretation: V ≈ 0.1 weak, 0.3 moderate, 0.5+ strong (varies with table dimensions). For 2×2 tables, V = |phi coefficient|.
When you see it
In feature importance screening for categorical variables, customer segment analysis, marketing mix evaluation, and any analysis asking how strongly two categorical factors are related.
Red flag
Ask whether a high Cramér's V is driven by a specific combination of categories rather than a general association. A V of 0.45 could reflect one dominant cell where two specific categories co-occur — which may be more interesting and actionable than a uniform association across all cells.
Business example
A retailer tests whether product category preference is associated with customer acquisition channel. Chi-square: χ²=142, p<0.001, V=0.31. Moderate association — online-acquired customers skew toward electronics and home; in-store customers skew toward clothing and food. The association is commercially meaningful for personalisation strategy.
Autocorrelation
Correlation of a variable with its own past values — a structural feature of time series data that violates many standard model assumptions.
expand
Intuition
It tells you today's value is correlated with yesterday's (or last week's, last year's) value. This is nearly universal in business time series — sales today predict sales tomorrow; this quarter's engagement predicts next quarter's. Autocorrelation violates the independence assumption of standard regression and inflates t-statistics, making everything look more significant than it is.
Formula / Rule
ACF(k) = Corr(yₜ, yₜ₋ₖ)ACF at lag k: correlation between observations k periods apart. Plot the ACF for lags 1 through 20 to identify structure. Positive ACF at lag 1 (most common) means high values tend to be followed by high values. Seasonal patterns show up as spikes at lag 7 (weekly), 12 (monthly), 52 (annual).
When you see it
In time series diagnostics, regression residual checks, A/B test sequential analysis, and any model using time-ordered observations where independence cannot be assumed.
Red flag
Ask whether the model's residuals were checked for autocorrelation. Autocorrelated residuals in a regression model indicate the model is missing time structure — standard errors are understated and significance tests are unreliable.
Business example
A grocery chain models daily sales using promotional spend as the sole predictor. R² = 0.84. Residual diagnostics show autocorrelation at lag 7 (ACF = 0.72) — weekly seasonal patterns dominate what the regression residuals look like. The model is largely fitting seasonal rhythm, not the promotional effect. A seasonal decomposition step is needed before isolating the true promotion contribution.
Section 8.2

The traps — spurious correlation, confounding, and Simpson's paradox

Correlation misleads in systematic, predictable ways. The traps below are not edge cases — they are encountered routinely in business data and consistently lead to wrong conclusions when not caught. Every correlation finding deserves to be checked against these failure modes before being acted on.

Spurious Correlation
Two variables appear correlated but share no causal connection — both are driven by a third variable or by chance.
expand
Intuition
It tells you that finding a correlation proves almost nothing about the relationship between two variables. US cheese consumption correlates with deaths by bedsheet tangling (r=0.947). Nicolas Cage film appearances correlate with pool drownings. These are not coincidences in any causal sense — they are mathematical patterns that can be generated by searching enough variable pairs. With modern datasets of thousands of variables, finding correlations above 0.7 by chance alone is trivially easy.
Formula / Rule
Two mechanisms generate spurious correlations: (1) Common cause — both variables are driven by a third factor (confounding). (2) Data dredging — searching many pairs until a high correlation appears by chance. Prevention: pre-specify hypotheses, require replication in out-of-sample data, and ask for a plausible causal mechanism before acting.
When you see it
In automated feature screening, algorithmic trading signals, marketing attribution models, and any analysis where correlations are discovered rather than pre-specified.
Red flag
Ask whether there is a credible causal mechanism linking the two variables. A high correlation without a plausible mechanism is a hypothesis requiring investigation, not a finding ready to act on.
Business example
A credit scoring team scans 3,000 candidate features and finds postcode has extremely high correlation with default. This may reflect real socioeconomic factors — or it may partly reflect the fact that with 3,000 variables screened, some spurious correlations will appear significant. The team validates the feature's predictive power on a held-out temporal sample before including it in the production model.
Confounding Variable
A variable that affects both the proposed cause and the outcome — creating the appearance of a causal relationship where one may not exist.
expand
Intuition
It tells you a hidden third variable may be driving both. Shoe size correlates with reading ability in children — but only because both are caused by age. Customer engagement score correlates with spending — but both are caused by a customer being highly valuable in the first place. Identifying and controlling for confounders is the central challenge of observational causal inference.
Formula / Rule
Variable C is a confounder of the X→Y relationship if: (1) C causes X, and (2) C causes Y, and (3) C is not on the causal path from X to Y. Controlling for C removes the spurious correlation. Randomization eliminates confounding by making treatment assignment independent of all potential confounders.
When you see it
In observational analysis of treatment effects, cohort studies, attribution modeling, and any analysis that attempts to establish a causal conclusion from non-experimental data.
Red flag
Ask what the most obvious confounder is for this relationship. For every claimed X→Y relationship, list three potential third variables that could cause both. If any of those confounders is not measured or controlled for, the causal claim is at risk.
Business example
A professional services firm finds that consultants who complete a training programme generate 40% more revenue in the following year. The obvious confounder: high performers are more likely to sign up for training (motivation and ability both drive selection into training and revenue performance). Without randomisation, the 40% gap may reflect who chose to train, not what training did to them.
Simpson's Paradox
A trend that appears in aggregate data reverses or disappears when the data is broken into subgroups — due to a confounding variable that determines group composition.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the aggregate result can tell the opposite story from the subgroup results. This is not a paradox in any deep sense — it is just a consequence of groups of different sizes containing different mixtures. The right level of analysis depends on what question you are asking: aggregate results answer population-level questions; subgroup results answer within-stratum questions.
Formula / Rule
Classic example: Treatment A cures 73% of kidney stones overall; Treatment B cures 83% overall. But for small stones: A=93%, B=87%. For large stones: A=73%, B=69%. Doctors assigned B more often to mild cases — the aggregate result favors B because of case mix, not efficacy. The subgroup result is the clinically correct one.
When you see it
In A/B tests with unequal traffic allocation across segments, channel performance comparisons, demographic analysis, and any aggregation across groups with very different compositions.
Red flag
Ask whether the two variants or groups being compared have meaningfully different compositions. If the distribution of a key confounding variable (customer type, traffic source, geography) differs between groups, aggregate comparison may be misleading.
Business example
A streaming service compares conversion rate between two homepage variants. Variant A: 4.8% conversion. Variant B: 4.2% conversion. Variant A looks better. But Variant A was shown to users from high-conversion organic search (40% of traffic) far more than Variant B. Segmenting by source: among organic search users, B converts at 6.1% vs A at 6.3% — essentially tied. Among all other traffic, B outperforms A. The aggregate result is entirely driven by traffic mix, not variant performance.
Ecological Fallacy
Applying group-level correlations to draw conclusions about individuals — a category error that produces systematically wrong inferences.
expand
Intuition
It tells you that correlations at the group level do not imply the same correlation at the individual level. Countries with higher chocolate consumption have more Nobel laureates — this correlation may reflect economic development driving both, but it tells you nothing about whether individual chocolate consumption predicts Nobel Prize potential. Group patterns emerge from group-level forces; individual behavior depends on individual-level factors.
Formula / Rule
If data is aggregated (regions, stores, cohorts), correlations computed from that data describe aggregate-level relationships — not within-unit individual relationships. To make individual-level claims, individual-level data is required. The appropriate analysis level is determined by the question being answered, not the convenience of the data available.
When you see it
In regional performance analysis, store-level benchmarking, cohort comparisons, and any business analysis using aggregate data to make claims about individual behavior.
Red flag
Ask whether the insight applies at the level of the individual decision (customer, transaction) or the aggregate (segment, region). An insight from store-level analysis may not hold at the transaction level — and vice versa.
Business example
A bank analyzes which postcodes have the highest default rates and considers restricting lending in those areas. The ecological analysis identifies community-level risk — but within high-default postcodes, the vast majority of individual borrowers repay. Restricting credit based on postcode denies creditworthy individuals on the basis of their neighbours' behaviour, and may expose the bank to fair lending regulatory risk.
Range Restriction
When a sample covers only part of the full variable range, correlations are systematically attenuated — creating underestimates of true relationships.
expand
Intuition
It tells you restricting the range of variation on one axis compresses the correlation toward zero, even if the true population relationship is strong. If you measure IQ-job performance correlation only among employees who passed a selection test (a restricted range of IQ), the observed correlation understates the relationship that exists in the full population. The same trap applies in credit models, hiring analytics, and any outcome measurement on a filtered population.
Formula / Rule
Range restriction attenuates r proportionally to how narrow the restricted range is. A variable with 50% of its natural variance removed will produce correlations approximately 25–40% smaller than the true population correlation. Correction: Thorndike formula or full-population data is required to recover the true relationship.
When you see it
In performance validation studies (data only on accepted applicants), marketing attribution (data only on customers who were contacted), and any outcome analysis on a screened population.
Red flag
Ask whether the analysis data covers the full range of the predictor. A credit model validated only on approved applicants cannot reliably predict performance for the declined population — the model has never seen those cases.
Business example
A lender validates its income-to-default model and finds a weak correlation (r=0.21) among approved applicants. A random sample experiment approving some normally-declined applicants reveals r=0.48 in the full population — range restriction (only approving mid-to-high income applicants) had compressed the measured relationship. The model was underusing income as a predictor because validation data was too narrow.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 8 cheat sheet

Key principles from this chapter
r measures linear relationships only
A perfect quadratic relationship can have r=0. Always plot before computing. Use Spearman for monotonic-but-nonlinear relationships or when outliers are present.
Correlation requires a plausible mechanism
With enough variables, spurious correlations are inevitable. Pre-specify hypotheses, require replication, and ask for the causal mechanism before acting on any discovered correlation.
Always check for confounders
The most dangerous correlations in business are those driven by a third variable nobody measured. List the most plausible confounders before drawing any causal conclusion.
Simpson's paradox is everywhere
Aggregate comparisons can reverse at subgroup level when group compositions differ. A/B test results should always be checked for composition effects before declaring a winner.
Group patterns ≠ individual patterns
Ecological correlations do not apply to individuals. Insights from store, region, or cohort data must be validated at the individual level before driving individual-level decisions.
Range restriction attenuates correlation
Models and correlations measured on filtered populations understate true relationships. Validation on screened samples gives optimistic (false) low-correlation estimates.
