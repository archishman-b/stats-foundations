Section 17.1

Common analytical workflows across business domains

The same statistical toolkit appears across different business domains under different names and with different operational stakes. Customer lifetime value, credit scoring, demand forecasting, and pricing elasticity are analytically analogous problems. Recognising the structure beneath the domain-specific vocabulary accelerates both learning and cross-functional communication.

Customer
Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)
Expected total net revenue from a customer over their relationship lifetime. Informs acquisition spend limits, retention investment, and segment-level strategy.
Regression + survival analysis + segmentation

Credit Scoring
Probability of default estimated from application and bureau data. Drives accept/decline decisions, pricing, and expected loss calculations for capital provisioning.

Attribution of revenue to marketing activities and external factors. Estimates media elasticities and ROI to guide budget allocation.

Pricing

Percentage change in demand for a 1% change in price. Estimates the revenue-optimal price point and informs promotional strategy.

Operations

Section 17.2

Customer lifetime value — the unifying customer metric

CLV is the single metric that connects customer acquisition, retention, and monetisation into one coherent framework. It converts behavioral predictions (will this customer churn? how much will they spend?) into financial projections that directly inform strategy. It is also the metric most commonly built on shaky statistical foundations — wrong discount rates, ignored censoring, or unstated horizon assumptions.

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)
The expected net present value of a customer's future contribution — the central metric for acquisition economics, retention investment, and portfolio strategy.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how much a customer relationship is worth in financial terms — enabling rational decisions about how much to spend acquiring, retaining, and serving them. A customer worth £4,200 CLV can support up to £4,200 in acquisition cost and still be profitable. Without CLV, acquisition budgets are set on unit economics that ignore long-run relationship value.
Formula / Rule
CLV = Σₜ (Revenue_t − Cost_t) × Retention_t / (1+r)ᵗKey components: expected revenue per period, expected cost to serve, probability of remaining a customer at each future period (survival function), and discount rate r. Three approaches: simple (margin × retention / (1 − retention + discount_rate)), probabilistic (BG/NBD model), and ML regression on historical outcomes.
When you see it
In acquisition economics (max CPA = CLV × margin%), retention ROI calculation (contact cost < CLV preserved × churn rate reduction), segmentation (value-based tiers), and M&A due diligence (customer asset valuation).
Red flag
Ask what horizon, discount rate, and retention curve were assumed. CLV is extremely sensitive to all three. A 24-month CLV with 10% annual discount and observed retention will differ 3–4× from a 60-month CLV with 5% discount and extrapolated retention. Always state assumptions explicitly.
Business example
A subscription software company calculates CLV across acquisition cohorts. Monthly revenue: £120. Monthly churn rate: 2.8%. Monthly cost to serve: £22. Discount rate: 8% annual. CLV = (£120−£22) × (1/(0.028+0.08/12)) = £98 / 0.0347 ≈ £2,825. The company can profitably spend up to £2,825 acquiring a customer who will generate average expected margin — 3.4× their current £825 average CPA, suggesting significant headroom to invest in growth channels.
Survival Analysis
Statistical framework for modeling time-to-event outcomes — accounting for censored observations where the event has not yet occurred.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how to analyze outcomes that involve both whether and when an event occurs. Standard regression discards censored observations (customers still active at the analysis date) — wasting information and biasing survival estimates. Survival analysis correctly handles the fact that an active customer provides information: they have survived at least this long.
Formula / Rule
S(t) = P(T > t) = exp(−∫₀ᵗ h(u)du)S(t) = survival function: probability of surviving past time t. h(t) = hazard rate: instantaneous rate of event at time t. Kaplan-Meier: non-parametric estimate of S(t) from data, handling censored observations correctly. Cox proportional hazards: semi-parametric model estimating how covariates scale the hazard rate while leaving baseline hazard unspecified.
When you see it
In subscription churn analysis (time-to-cancel), credit default modeling (time-to-default), equipment maintenance (time-to-failure), clinical trials (time-to-event), and customer cohort analyses where time dimension matters.
Red flag
Ask whether the analysis used survival methods or simply discarded censored observations. Dropping active customers from churn analysis produces biased estimates — typically overstating churn rate by excluding the survivors.
Business example
A SaaS company analyzes 12-month subscription retention. Standard logistic regression classifying "churned by 12 months" drops the 40% of customers still active at analysis date — biasing estimates toward shorter-tenured customers. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis correctly incorporates all customers, estimating 12-month survival at 68% — a materially different estimate from the naive 48% from the biased logistic approach.
Anomaly Detection
Identifying observations that deviate significantly from expected patterns — used in fraud, quality control, cybersecurity, and operational monitoring.
expand
Intuition
It tells you something has occurred that the normal-operation model does not explain. Anomaly detection is distinct from classification: in classification, you know what the anomalous class looks like and can learn from examples. In anomaly detection, you often have only normal data and must identify deviations from it — making it inherently one-class.
Formula / Rule
Statistical: flag observations beyond ±3 SD or past Q3+3×IQR. Isolation Forest: anomaly score based on average path length in random trees — anomalous points are isolated with fewer splits. DBSCAN: points not belonging to any dense cluster are anomalies. PSI and rolling z-score for time series metric monitoring. Contamination rate: the assumed fraction of anomalies in the dataset (typically 0.5–5%).
When you see it
In fraud detection, network intrusion, manufacturing defect identification, KPI monitoring dashboards, and any operational system requiring automated identification of unusual events.
Red flag
Ask what contamination rate was assumed and whether it was validated. Isolation Forest requires specifying the expected fraction of anomalies — setting this too high flags normal operations as anomalous, overwhelming analysts with false alarms.
Business example
A payment network's transaction monitoring uses Isolation Forest with contamination=0.002 (0.2% expected fraud rate, calibrated on historical data). Daily alert volume: ~1,400 transactions from 700,000 processed. Investigation confirms 38% of alerts are genuine fraud (precision 0.38 — see Chapter 13). The contamination rate calibration directly determines the operational alert burden on the investigations team.
Price Elasticity
The percentage change in demand for a 1% change in price — the core statistic for pricing strategy and revenue optimization.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how sensitive demand is to price changes. Elasticity of −2.3 means a 1% price increase reduces demand by 2.3% (elastic — price increase reduces revenue). Elasticity of −0.6 means a 1% price increase reduces demand by only 0.6% (inelastic — price increase raises revenue). Understanding elasticity is the foundation of evidence-based pricing — without it, price changes are guesses.
Formula / Rule
ε = % change in quantity / % change in price = (∂Q/Q) / (∂P/P)Estimated via log-log regression: log(Q) = α + ε×log(P) + controls. |ε| > 1: elastic (price increase reduces revenue). |ε| < 1: inelastic (price increase raises revenue). Revenue-maximising price is where ε = −1. Causal identification requires a price instrument or randomised price experiment — observational price variation is often confounded by demand-driven pricing.
When you see it
In pricing strategy, revenue management, promotional evaluation, and the marketing mix model's price coefficient.
Red flag
Ask whether the elasticity estimate was corrected for endogeneity. If prices were set higher when demand was high (standard revenue management), the naive regression estimate understates the true price sensitivity. Instrumental variables or randomised pricing experiments are needed for credible causal estimates.
Business example
A grocery retailer estimates price elasticity for premium cereal using log-log regression with store fixed effects: ε = −1.84. Current price: £4.20. Revenue-maximising price: where ε = −1, estimated at approximately £2.90 — substantially below current price, suggesting premium pricing is above the revenue-maximising point. The team runs a randomised price test across 120 stores to validate before any rollout.
Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM)
A time series regression that attributes revenue to marketing inputs and external factors — estimating channel ROI and budget allocation recommendations.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how much of observed sales came from TV, digital, promotions, price changes, and macro factors — versus baseline. By estimating each channel's contribution and the shape of its response curve (typically with diminishing returns), MMM directly informs how to allocate the marketing budget to maximise revenue per pound spent.
Formula / Rule
Sales_t = baseline + Σ β_c × f(spend_c,t) + γ × seasonality + ε_tf(spend) typically = Adstock transformation (exponentially decayed carry-over effect) + saturation function (Hill/diminishing returns). Bayesian MMM: incorporates prior knowledge about elasticities and carry-over to stabilise estimates in the presence of multicollinearity. Requires 2–3 years of weekly data minimum.
When you see it
In annual media planning, budget allocation reviews, "what-if" scenario modeling for channel mix changes, and brand effectiveness measurement.
Red flag
Ask whether the model was validated with holdout tests. MMM collinearity between channels means attribution is often non-unique — multiple channel combinations can explain the same historical data equally well. Holdout experiments on specific channels provide causal validation that the model-derived attributions cannot.
Business example
A UK FMCG brand runs a Bayesian MMM on 3 years of weekly data. Results: TV ROI = 1.42x, digital ROI = 2.18x, OOH ROI = 1.05x. Budget reallocation from OOH (−£2M) to digital (+£2M) is projected to increase attributed revenue by £2.3M. Before implementing, the team runs an incrementality test — suppressing digital in a matched market group — to validate the digital elasticity estimate. Results confirm 1.9× ROI, close to model estimate.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 17 cheat sheet

Key principles from this chapter
CLV requires explicit assumptions
Horizon, discount rate, and retention curve assumptions change CLV by 3–4×. Always state them. CLV comparisons across studies are meaningless unless assumptions match.
Survival analysis for time-to-event
Discarding censored observations (active customers) biases churn and retention estimates. Kaplan-Meier and Cox correctly handles the fact that "still active" is informative data.
Price elasticity requires causal identification
Naive regression on price confounds demand-driven pricing with price sensitivity. Randomised price tests or instrumental variables are needed for credible elasticity estimates.
Anomaly detection requires contamination calibration
Setting the expected anomaly rate (contamination) determines alert volume. Too high = analyst overload from false alarms. Calibrate on historical confirmed anomaly rates.
MMM attribution requires holdout validation
Collinearity between channels makes model-derived attribution non-unique. Validate high-stakes channel elasticities with geo-level holdout experiments before budget reallocation.
Match method to business decision
Prediction for targeting → ML + AUC. Causal attribution for ROI → RCT/MMM/DiD. Segmentation for strategy → clustering + interpretability review. Survival for time-valued metrics → Kaplan-Meier + Cox.
