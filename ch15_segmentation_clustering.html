Segmentation and Clustering

Clustering finds structure in data without a predefined outcome — grouping customers, products, or markets by similarity. It is one of the most used and most misused tools in business analytics, because there is no ground truth to validate against, and almost any algorithm will produce clusters whether meaningful ones exist or not.

Terms covered: 16 Sections: K-means · Hierarchical · DBSCAN · Evaluation · Dimensionality reduction · PCA
Section 15.1

Clustering algorithms — the right tool for the structure you expect

Different clustering algorithms make different assumptions about cluster shape, size, and number. Choosing the algorithm before exploring the data structure is a common mistake — the algorithm should match what the data actually looks like, not what is most convenient to implement.

Centroid-based
K-Means
Partitions data into k spherical clusters by iteratively assigning points to nearest centroid and updating centroids. Fast, scalable, requires k specified upfront.
Best for: large tabular datasets, equal-size approximately spherical clusters
Hierarchy-based
Hierarchical (Ward)
Builds a dendrogram by successively merging (agglomerative) or splitting (divisive) clusters. No k required upfront — cut the dendrogram at chosen depth.
Best for: small-medium datasets, exploring cluster structure, variable-size clusters
Density-based
DBSCAN
Finds dense regions separated by low-density areas. Handles arbitrary shapes, automatically identifies noise/outliers as points belonging to no cluster.
Best for: geographic/spatial data, irregular cluster shapes, anomaly identification
Probabilistic
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM)
Models data as a mixture of Gaussian distributions. Returns soft cluster assignments (probabilities). Handles elliptical clusters of different sizes.
Best for: overlapping clusters, uncertainty quantification, when membership should be probabilistic
K-Means Clustering
Partitions data into k clusters by minimising within-cluster variance — the most widely used clustering algorithm in business analytics.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the algorithm is finding k groups where observations within each group are as similar to each other (and as different from other groups) as possible. K-means defines "similar" as close in Euclidean distance to the cluster centroid — which means it assumes clusters are roughly spherical, similar in size, and the features are on comparable scales.
Formula / Rule
Minimize: Σ_k Σ_{i∈Cₖ} ||xᵢ − μₖ||²K-means++ initialization (random seeding far from existing centroids) reduces poor convergence. Always run 10+ random starts — results vary by initialization. Scale features before K-means (distance-based, scale-sensitive). Use elbow plot or silhouette score to choose k.
When you see it
In customer segmentation, product portfolio clustering, geographic market grouping, and any business context requiring discrete, interpretable groups from continuous features.
Red flag
Ask whether features were standardised before clustering. K-means on unstandardised features will create clusters dominated by the highest-variance variable — income (range £20k–£200k) will swamp age (range 18–80) and the clustering will essentially just be income-based regardless of other features.
Business example
A telecoms operator clusters 4M customers on 15 standardised behavioral features (spend, usage, tenure, channel). K-means with k=6 (selected by silhouette score) identifies: high-value tenured (23%), digital-native mid-value (19%), heavy-data mobile-first (17%), price-sensitive churners (15%), voice-primary seniors (14%), low-engagement dormant (12%). Each segment gets differentiated retention, pricing, and acquisition strategy.
Hierarchical Clustering
Builds a tree of nested clusters (dendrogram) — letting analysts choose the level of granularity without re-running the algorithm.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the data contains nested structure — that what looks like one segment at the strategic level may be several distinct micro-segments at the operational level. Hierarchical clustering captures this without requiring k to be specified upfront and allows exploring multiple resolutions of the same structure through the dendrogram.
Formula / Rule
Agglomerative: start with each point as its own cluster, iteratively merge the two most similar. Ward linkage minimises within-cluster variance at each merge — best for compact, equal-sized clusters. Single linkage = minimum distance (prone to chaining). Complete linkage = maximum distance (compact clusters). Cut the dendrogram at the largest gap in merge distances to find natural k.
When you see it
In market structure analysis, product taxonomy development, brand portfolio rationalization, and any business question where understanding the hierarchy of similarity matters as much as the final grouping.
Red flag
Ask what linkage criterion was used. Single linkage is prone to "chaining" — one very large elongated cluster forms while others are tiny. Ward linkage generally produces the most interpretable, compact customer segments for business applications.
Business example
A consumer goods company uses hierarchical clustering on 200 SKUs by flavor, format, and price point to rationalise the portfolio. The dendrogram reveals a natural 3-cluster top-level structure (premium, mainstream, value) with 12 sub-clusters within those. This hierarchical structure informs both brand architecture decisions and SKU reduction targeting — eliminating sub-clusters with overlapping positioning.
Soft Cluster Assignments (GMM)
Probabilistic membership — each observation belongs to all clusters with varying probabilities, rather than exclusively to one.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the model is honest about boundary cases. A customer with 62% probability in Cluster A and 38% in Cluster B is genuinely intermediate — forcing hard assignment to A misrepresents how similar they are to B. Gaussian Mixture Models produce these soft assignments by fitting a probabilistic model where each cluster is a Gaussian distribution with its own mean and covariance.
Formula / Rule
P(cluster k | xᵢ) = πₖ × N(xᵢ|μₖ,Σₖ) / Σⱼ πⱼ × N(xᵢ|μⱼ,Σⱼ)Fitted by Expectation-Maximisation (EM). Use BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) to select number of components. Covariance type (full, tied, diagonal, spherical) controls cluster shape flexibility — full is most flexible, spherical is equivalent to soft K-means.
When you see it
In customer lifetime value modeling where customer types overlap, document topic modeling (LDA is a GMM variant), and any segmentation where boundary uncertainty is commercially important.
Red flag
Ask how uncertain assignments are handled operationally. Soft assignments are statistically honest but operationally inconvenient — most CRM systems require a hard segment label. Define a business rule for edge cases (e.g. assign to the highest-probability cluster, but flag cases below 60% as "undecided" for manual review).
Business example
A bank clusters customers for product recommendation. GMM identifies 5 segments. 18% of customers have no cluster with probability above 60% — they sit genuinely between segments. These "multi-segment" customers receive a hybrid product recommendation rather than being forced into the nearest segment's default recommendation — improving cross-sell conversion by 14% for this group.
Section 15.2

Evaluating cluster quality — without ground truth

Unlike supervised models, clustering has no ground truth to validate against. Evaluation must rely on internal cohesion metrics (are similar things being grouped together?) and business interpretability (do the segments mean something actionable?). Statistical and business validity are both necessary — neither alone is sufficient.

Silhouette Score
Measures how similar each point is to its own cluster versus neighbouring clusters — the standard internal clustering quality metric.
expand
Intuition
It tells you how well each observation fits its assigned cluster. A score near +1 means the observation is much closer to its own cluster than to the nearest alternative — well-assigned. A score near 0 means it sits on the boundary between two clusters. A score near −1 means it may be assigned to the wrong cluster.
Formula / Rule
s(i) = (b(i) − a(i)) / max(a(i), b(i))a(i) = mean distance to other points in same cluster (cohesion). b(i) = mean distance to points in nearest other cluster (separation). Range: [−1, +1]. Mean silhouette ≥ 0.50 is generally considered good clustering structure. Use to compare different k values — select k with highest mean silhouette.
When you see it
In k-selection analysis (elbow vs silhouette comparison), cluster quality reporting, and validation of whether a proposed segmentation has statistical coherence before investing in strategic interpretation.
Red flag
Ask whether the silhouette score was computed on the training data or on a held-out validation set. On training data, silhouette is optimistically biased — the algorithm fits to maximise cohesion. Validate on a held-out 20% to get an honest quality estimate.
Business example
A retailer tests k=3 through k=10 for customer clustering. Silhouette scores: k=4 (0.48), k=5 (0.56), k=6 (0.53), k=7 (0.51). k=5 maximises silhouette at 0.56. A business review of the 5 segments confirms interpretability — each has a distinct actionable strategy. The combination of statistical quality (silhouette) and business interpretability validates k=5.
Scree Plot and Elbow Method
Plots within-cluster variance (or explained variance for PCA) against k — the "elbow" indicates where adding more clusters yields diminishing returns.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the natural breaking point in complexity — where the return from adding another cluster (or component) drops sharply. The elbow is where the curve changes from steep to flat. Before the elbow, each additional cluster explains significant new variation; after the elbow, clusters are splitting existing coherent groups rather than capturing genuinely new structure.
Formula / Rule
For K-means: plot within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS or inertia) vs k. The elbow is the point of maximum curvature. For PCA: plot explained variance % vs number of components. Limitation: the elbow is often subjective — the plot may not have a clear bend. Use alongside silhouette score and business interpretability as a multi-criterion decision.
When you see it
In clustering k-selection analysis and PCA component selection — always as a visual tool to guide, not determine, the final number.
Red flag
Ask whether a clear elbow was visible or whether k was chosen subjectively from a continuously decreasing curve. When the elbow is ambiguous, rely more heavily on silhouette score and business interpretability to make the final selection.
Business example
An insurer clusters policyholders for risk stratification. Scree plot shows WCSS decreasing steeply from k=1 to k=4, then flattening at k=5. Silhouette peaks at k=4 (0.61). Business review confirms 4 interpretable risk profiles (standard, above-average, high-risk, very high-risk). Both methods agree — k=4 is the right choice.
Section 15.3

Dimensionality reduction — PCA and related methods

High-dimensional data is hard to cluster, visualise, and model. Dimensionality reduction compresses many correlated features into a smaller set of uncorrelated components while preserving most of the information. It is a preprocessing step for clustering, a visualisation tool for exploration, and a method for removing multicollinearity before regression.

PCA — Principal Component Analysis
Finds orthogonal linear combinations of features that explain the maximum variance — reducing dimensionality while preserving information.
expand
Intuition
It tells you a new set of uncorrelated "super-features" (principal components) that summarise the original variables. PC1 explains the most variance, PC2 the second most, and so on — each orthogonal to all others. If 10 correlated features can be summarised by 3 PCs explaining 85% of the variance, those 3 PCs are often more useful for clustering and modeling than the 10 originals.
Formula / Rule
PCA = eigenvectors of covariance matrixStandardise features before PCA (otherwise high-variance features dominate). Use scree plot to select number of components — typically retain enough to explain 70–85% of variance. Component loadings: the correlation of each original feature with each PC — use to name and interpret what each component represents.
When you see it
As preprocessing before clustering (reduces noise, speeds up algorithms), in data visualisation (project to 2D for scatterplot), and in regression when multicollinearity must be removed without feature selection.
Red flag
Ask whether PCA components are interpretable for the business use case. PCA is optimal for variance compression but produces components that are linear combinations of all features — sometimes hard to explain to stakeholders. If interpretability is required, factor analysis or targeted feature selection may be preferable.
Business example
A credit bureau has 85 credit bureau variables per applicant, highly intercorrelated. PCA reduces to 8 components explaining 79% of total variance. The top component loads heavily on payment history and delinquency variables — interpretable as "payment reliability." The second loads on utilisation and balance variables — "debt burden." These components become clean, uncorrelated inputs to the credit risk model, eliminating multicollinearity while retaining most information.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 15 cheat sheet

Key principles from this chapter
Match algorithm to expected structure
K-means for spherical, equal-size clusters. Hierarchical for exploring nested structure. DBSCAN for arbitrary shapes and outlier detection. GMM when cluster boundaries should be soft.
Always standardise before clustering
Distance-based algorithms (K-means, hierarchical) are dominated by the highest-variance feature if features are unstandardised. Scale everything to mean 0, SD 1 first.
Evaluate with silhouette + business review
Statistical quality (silhouette ≥ 0.5 is good) is necessary but not sufficient. Every segment must be interpretable, actionably different, and large enough to warrant a distinct strategy.
Use scree/elbow + silhouette to choose k
Neither alone is decisive. Use both, plus ask: does each k-segment have a distinct business profile? The right k is the smallest number that captures all commercially meaningful differences.
PCA before clustering reduces noise
High-dimensional data with correlated features → cluster on first 5–10 PCs rather than raw features. Reduces computational cost and suppresses noise variables that dilute cluster quality.
Soft assignments for boundary cases
Customers near cluster boundaries may genuinely be intermediate. GMM captures this honestly. Define an operational rule for how to treat borderline cases (usually assign to highest probability, flag below 60% for review).
