Time Series and Forecasting

Time series data is different from cross-sectional data in one fundamental way: order matters. Shuffling the observations destroys information. This chapter covers decomposition, stationarity, ARIMA, structural breaks, and the operational discipline of forecasting in production environments.

Terms covered: 18 Sections: Decomposition · Stationarity · ARIMA · Structural breaks · Seasonality · Forecasting methods
Section 16.1

Time series decomposition — separating what changes from why

Before modeling a time series, decompose it. Trend, seasonality, and noise are fundamentally different phenomena driven by different factors and requiring different handling. Conflating them produces forecasts that confuse structural change with seasonal patterns and mistake noise for signal.

STL decomposition separates a time series into trend (long-run direction), seasonal (recurring pattern), and residual (noise). The trend and seasonal components are modeled; the residual should look like white noise.
Time Series Decomposition
Separating a time series into trend, seasonal, and residual components — the essential first step before any forecasting work.
expand
Intuition
It tells you which part of what you see in the data is structural, which is periodic, and which is noise. Revenue increasing every December is seasonal — not growth. Revenue declining steadily over 18 months is trend — not a bad December. Separating these before modeling prevents misattribution of seasonal highs to strategic success and seasonal lows to operational failure.
Formula / Rule
Additive: Y = Trend + Seasonal + ResidualMultiplicative: Y = Trend × Seasonal × Residual (when seasonal amplitude scales with level — common for revenue). STL decomposition (Seasonal-Trend using Loess) is the most robust method — handles multiple seasonalities, outliers, and changing seasonal patterns. Use additive for stable amplitude; multiplicative for growing amplitude.
When you see it
In every production forecasting system before building models, in attribution analyses separating baseline from promotional effects, and in any business review comparing year-over-year performance.
Red flag
Ask whether the team decomposed the series before declaring trend changes. A business reporting "revenue grew 23% in Q4" may be entirely attributable to seasonal Q4 patterns — year-over-year comparison on seasonally adjusted data is the appropriate metric.
Business example
A fashion retailer's raw monthly revenue shows apparent strong growth. STL decomposition reveals: trend is flat; seasonal component explains 89% of observed variation; residuals are small and structurally well-behaved. The "growth" is December seasonality repeated annually. Year-over-year seasonally adjusted comparison shows no underlying trend growth — a very different strategy implication.
Stationarity
A time series is stationary when its statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation structure) do not change over time — required by most classical forecasting models.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the series is well-behaved enough for standard time series methods. A trending series is not stationary — its mean is changing. A series with increasing volatility is not stationary — its variance is changing. ARIMA models require stationarity and use differencing to achieve it. Neural network models are more tolerant but still benefit from trend removal.
Formula / Rule
ADF test: H₀: unit root (non-stationary). Low p-value → stationaryAugmented Dickey-Fuller test: p < 0.05 means the series is stationary. If non-stationary: first difference (yₜ − yₜ₋₁) often achieves stationarity. Log-transform first if variance is growing. Second-difference if first difference is still non-stationary. Never over-difference — it introduces unnecessary complexity.
When you see it
In ARIMA model specification, time series regression diagnostics, and the pre-modeling checklist for any sequential data analysis.
Red flag
Ask whether the stationarity test was conducted before fitting the model. Fitting ARIMA to a trending series without differencing produces spurious results — the model will find apparent relationships that are entirely driven by shared trends rather than true dependence.
Business example
An analyst regresses website traffic on ad spend using both series in levels (not differenced). Both series trend upward over 3 years. The regression finds a strong positive relationship (R²=0.87) — but a spurious regression: any two trending series will appear correlated. ADF tests on both series confirm non-stationarity. First-differencing both series (week-over-week changes) reduces R² to 0.31 — the true short-run relationship between ad spend changes and traffic changes.
ARIMA
AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average — the classical framework for univariate time series forecasting using lagged values and lagged errors.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the model predicts future values using a combination of: past values of the series itself (autoregressive part), the number of differences needed for stationarity (integrated part), and past forecast errors (moving average part). ARIMA is optimal for linear time series with stable statistical properties and no external predictors.
Formula / Rule
ARIMA(p,d,q): p=AR lags, d=differences, q=MA lagsUse ADF test to determine d. Use ACF/PACF plots to guide p and q. Seasonal ARIMA: SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)[s] adds seasonal AR, differencing, and MA terms at lag s. In practice: auto.arima() in R or pmdarima in Python searches the order space automatically. Evaluate with AIC/BIC for model selection.
When you see it
In economic forecasting, financial time series, energy demand modeling, and any univariate series with sufficient history and stable structure.
Red flag
Ask whether residuals were checked after fitting. ARIMA residuals should be white noise — if significant autocorrelation remains, the model is misspecified and forecasts are suboptimal. Check with Ljung-Box test.
Business example
A utility models monthly gas consumption. ADF test confirms non-stationarity. Auto.arima selects SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1)[12] — first-differenced, seasonal period 12 months. Model MAPE on held-out 12 months: 6.8% vs naive seasonal benchmark of 11.2%. Residuals pass Ljung-Box test. The SARIMA captures the serial autocorrelation and annual seasonality meaningfully better than naive methods.
Structural Break
A point in a time series where the underlying data-generating process fundamentally changes — invalidating any model trained on pre-break data.
expand
Intuition
It tells you the world changed in a way that makes pre-break data poor predictors of post-break outcomes. COVID-19, a new competitor entry, a regulatory change, or a pricing restructure can all create structural breaks. A model trained entirely on pre-break data will not just be slightly inaccurate — its core learned patterns may be reversed or irrelevant.
Formula / Rule
Chow test: F-test for parameter equality across two subperiodsDetect: Chow test at known break date, or Bai-Perron test to detect unknown break dates. Response: (1) Exclude pre-break data and retrain. (2) Include a break dummy variable if break is partial. (3) Use models robust to breaks (shorter training windows, regime-switching models). Rolling validation is critical for detecting breaks in production.
When you see it
In model performance diagnostics after major market events, regulatory changes, or business disruptions, and in any model governance process for models trained on multi-year historical data.
Red flag
Ask whether a model trained before a major structural event was revalidated (not just monitored) after the event. Post-event revalidation with a statistical break test is more reliable than watching performance metrics degrade.
Business example
A hospitality company's occupancy forecasting model was trained on 2015–2019 data. COVID-19 created a structural break in March 2020. The model assumes pre-pandemic seasonality, travel patterns, and baseline demand — all invalidated. Even post-reopening in 2022, behavioural shifts (remote work, different travel patterns) mean the pre-break data is contaminated. The model is rebuilt on 2021–2023 data with a shorter training window explicitly chosen to exclude the break period.
Prophet and Modern Forecasting Frameworks
Decomposable forecasting models designed for business time series — handling multiple seasonalities, holidays, and structural breaks with minimal tuning.
expand
Intuition
It tells you business time series have features that classical ARIMA handles poorly: multiple seasonalities (weekly and annual simultaneously), irregular holidays, known changepoints, and missing values. Facebook's Prophet and similar frameworks explicitly decompose into trend + seasonalities + holidays, fitting each component by curve-fitting rather than differencing. They are more robust to irregularities and more interpretable than black-box ML forecasters.
Formula / Rule
y(t) = g(t) + s(t) + h(t) + ε(t)g(t) = piecewise linear or logistic trend with changepoints. s(t) = Fourier series seasonality (multiple periods). h(t) = holiday effects (user-specified). Fit by Stan (Bayesian). Key advantage: handles daily data with weekly + annual seasonality naturally. Changepoints can be manually specified for known structural breaks.
When you see it
In retail, media, SaaS, and e-commerce forecasting where daily/weekly granularity and business calendar effects matter and classical ARIMA would require extensive manual specification.
Red flag
Ask whether Prophet's uncertainty intervals were validated for coverage. Prophet provides prediction intervals by default, but these are based on assumed uncertainty propagation — empirical coverage should be checked on held-out data before trusting them for safety stock or risk planning.
Business example
A food delivery platform forecasts daily order volume across 50 cities. Each city has weekly cycles, national holidays, and local events. Prophet fits each city independently, automatically capturing weekly seasonality and national holiday effects specified in the holiday calendar. MAPE across cities: 8.4% vs SARIMA benchmark of 12.1%. The improvement comes from handling multiple seasonalities simultaneously rather than the structural flexibility of the framework.
Chapter Summary

Chapter 16 cheat sheet

Key principles from this chapter
Decompose before modeling
Always separate trend, seasonality, and residuals before any forecasting work. STL decomposition handles multiple seasonalities and outliers robustly.
Test for stationarity before ARIMA
ADF test determines d (differencing order). Fitting ARIMA to a trending series without differencing produces spurious results and unreliable forecasts.
Check ARIMA residuals for white noise
Ljung-Box test on residuals. Remaining autocorrelation means the model is misspecified and leaving predictable structure unexploited.
Structural breaks invalidate training data
After major market events, don't just monitor — revalidate with a Chow test or Bai-Perron test. Pre-break data may be more harmful than helpful for post-break forecasting.
Use temporal cross-validation
Never use random k-fold on time series. Always train on past, evaluate on future — with expanding or rolling windows. Random splits create look-ahead bias.
Prophet for multi-seasonality business series
When daily data has both weekly and annual seasonality plus holiday effects, Prophet's explicit decomposition outperforms classical ARIMA with far less manual specification.
